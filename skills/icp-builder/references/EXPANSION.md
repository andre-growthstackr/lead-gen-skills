# ICP Expansion Framework
*Source: The Science of Scaling with Mark Roberge, HubSpot Media*

Load this file when the user is Series B+ or explicitly asks about expanding their
ICP, moving upmarket, entering new markets, or scaling beyond their initial segment.

---

## The Core Tension: Focus vs. Growth

The ICP that gets you to $1–5M in revenue likely won't get you to $50M and beyond.
At some point, expansion becomes necessary. But most companies expand wrong — chasing
inbound demand or big logos rather than running disciplined experiments.

> "Don't just chase whoever comes to you. If you're trying to sell to everyone who
> shows interest, you're not going to be strategic, you're not going to be successful.
> You're going to end up boiling the ocean." — Mark Roberge

**The disciplined path:** Use 90% of resources to deepen penetration of the proven
ICP. Use 10% on structured expansion experiments — defined in advance, before you
desperately need new growth.

---

## The 90/10 Resource Allocation Model

| Allocation | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **90%** | Execute against the proven, validated ICP — deepen penetration, improve retention, expand existing accounts |
| **10%** | Run structured expansion experiments into adjacent TAMs, new segments, or new geographies |

**Critical:** Define the 10% experiments *before* you need new growth, not after you've
hit a wall. Companies that plan expansion experiments proactively avoid the desperation
moves that dilute the core ICP.

## Types of ICP Expansion

### 1. Division Hopping (Lowest Risk)
Land in one part of an organization, then expand to other divisions. You already have
a relationship, a track record, and organizational credibility. This is often the
easiest and highest-ROI expansion path.

Example: Sell to the marketing team → expand to sales ops → expand to product.

### 2. Moving Upmarket (Medium Risk)
Expand from SMB to mid-market, or from mid-market to enterprise. Higher ACV, longer
sales cycles, more complex procurement and security requirements.

Key considerations:
- Enterprise buyers have different decision-making structures — more stakeholders,
  longer timelines, procurement and legal involvement
- Your product may need additional features (SSO, admin controls, compliance)
- Requires modified pitch, new comp structures, and potentially dedicated reps
- Multi-year contracts become standard; this changes how you model LTV

### 3. New TAM Expansion (Highest Risk)
Entering a completely new market segment — different industry, different buyer profile,
different use case. Treat this like starting a new business unit: you are essentially
restarting product-market fit and go-to-market fit from scratch.

> "Expansion into a new TAM requires restarting PMF and GTM fit from scratch.
> Plan accordingly." — Mark Roberge

## The 9-Part Expansion Experiment Template

Use this template when a user is planning a structured ICP expansion. Walk through
each section with them.

### 1. Objective
What do you want to learn or achieve with this experiment? Be specific. Focus on
learning objectives, not just revenue targets.

Example: *"Evaluate whether our project management platform can serve marketing
agencies in addition to software companies, with a goal of 3 closed deals and clear
signal on fit within one quarter."*

### 2. Target TAM / ICP
Define the new segment precisely. Don't say "larger companies" — give parameters:
- **Industry:** "SaaS companies in HR technology"
- **Company size:** "1,000–5,000 employees, $100M+ revenue"
- **Geography:** "UK and Ireland initially, then Western Europe"
- **Channel:** "Industry-specific consultants who advise on tech implementation"

Also articulate *why* this segment looks promising. Inbound signals? Market research?
Patterns in customer conversations? Ground the experiment in evidence.

### 3. Hypothesis
State what you believe to be true — specifically enough that it's disprovable.

Example: *"We believe companies in the renewable energy sector with 100+ employees
have a significant unmet need for our solution. We expect strong engagement in demos,
shorter sales cycles than our current average, and contract values 20%+ above our
core ICP."*

Your hypothesis should include:
- The unmet need or unique characteristic in the target segment
- Your value proposition for this segment
- Why you expect this to work differently than with your core ICP

### 4. Experiment Design
Define exactly how you'll test the hypothesis:

- **Dedicated resources:** Who specifically is running this — name and % time commitment
- **Targeted outreach:** How many target companies, what channels, what events
- **Sales approach:** How the pitch will be modified for this segment
- **Partnerships:** Any channel partners, resellers, or consultancies to engage

Avoid extremes: too small and you won't learn anything meaningful; too large and you
divert resources from the core ICP at real cost.

### 5. Resource Allocation
Put real numbers to the 10% commitment:

- **Budget:** Specific dollar amount for marketing activities
- **Headcount:** Named rep(s), % of time committed
- **Product:** Engineering hours available if product gaps emerge
- **Sales engineering:** Hours per week available for support

Articulating this creates accountability and ensures the experiment is meaningful
without threatening the core business.

### 6. Key Metrics for Success
Don't measure everything — focus on the indicators that signal real traction:

- **Lead generation:** Target number of qualified leads from the new segment
- **Meeting conversion:** Discovery meetings booked and % of outreach that converts
- **Pipeline development:** Dollar value of qualified pipeline with deals progressing
- **Win rate:** Number of pilot deals closed with defined success parameters
- **Customer success:** First customers achieving defined success metrics within 60 days
- **Expansion indicators:** Early interest in upsells or referrals within 90 days
- **LTV signal:** Initial contract values vs. core ICP average

### 7. Timeline
Create a schedule with clear milestones and forced decision points:

- **Week 1–2:** Market research, messaging development, outreach list creation
- **Week 3–6:** First wave of outreach, content creation, initial meetings
- **Week 7–10:** Follow-up meetings, proposal development, partnership discussions
- **Week 11–12:** Data analysis and recommendation preparation
- **Week 13+:** Decision point — expand, refine, or terminate

A defined timeline prevents experiments from dragging on indefinitely and forces a
moment of honest reckoning.

### 8. Learning Objectives
Beyond success metrics, define the specific insights you want regardless of revenue outcome:

- How do pain points in this segment differ from your core ICP?
- What does the buying committee look like — who's involved and who has veto power?
- What's the competitive landscape — what are they currently using?
- What's the typical sales cycle, and where do deals stall?
- What pricing adjustments would this segment require?

These learnings are valuable even if the experiment doesn't generate immediate revenue.
They inform product development, future experiments, and your core ICP.

### 9. Decision Points
Define clear criteria for next steps before the experiment begins:

| Result | Decision |
|---|---|
| >90% of metric targets hit | Full scale-up: invest in dedicated team and expanded marketing |
| 70–90% of targets hit | Extend experiment one quarter with incremental resource increase |
| 40–70% with promising sub-segment | Pivot: refocus experiment on the bright spot |
| <40% with no clear signal | Terminate: reallocate resources to core ICP |

Also define how you'll communicate outcomes to the broader org and investors —
regardless of whether the experiment continues.

## Case Study: Seismic

**Ed Calnan**, founding CRO of Seismic, exemplifies disciplined ICP targeting. During
early growth, he deliberately targeted mid-market companies interested in working with
emerging vendors — specifically because those buyers want to influence how the platform
evolves. This gave Seismic a concentrated base of early advocates who became references
and shaped the product.

The lesson: customers who came in via warm referrals or champion introductions —
without a tight ICP match — often didn't stick. Once Seismic found their sweet spot,
they laser-focused on it.

> "Those logos tended to go away. And once we found our sweet spot, we laser focused
> on it." — Ed Calnan, Founding CRO of Seismic

## When to Have the Expansion Conversation

Prompt the user to think about expansion if:
- They're Series B or later and growing consistently within their core ICP
- They have ~$10M+ ARR and 2–3 years of renewal data (LTV ICP analysis is now possible)
- They're hearing consistent inbound signals from an adjacent segment
- Growth in the core ICP is plateauing

Don't push expansion thinking on early-stage companies trying to nail their first ICP.
The focus/constraint is the advantage at that stage.
