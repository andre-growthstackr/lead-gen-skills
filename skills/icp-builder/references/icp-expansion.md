# ICP Expansion Reference

This file contains frameworks and principles for helping users think beyond their initial ICP.
Load this into context when a user's ICP is already partially defined and they're asking about
growing beyond their current market, expanding to enterprise, going upmarket, or entering new segments.

---

## The 90/10 Resource Allocation Model

Once a company has proven product-market fit within a core ICP:

- **90% of resources** → exploit the known, proven ICP (salespeople, marketing spend, product dev)
- **10% of resources** → run structured expansion experiments into new ICPs, segments, or geos

This prevents both stagnation (never expanding) and overextension (boiling the ocean prematurely).

---

## Signs the Core ICP May Be Approaching Saturation

Watch for these signals that it's time to consider expansion:

- Slowing growth rate within the established ICP
- Increasing customer acquisition costs within the core segment
- Diminishing returns from marketing spend in the initial market
- Difficulty reaching ambitious revenue goals with current market alone
- Expansion revenue from existing accounts outpacing new logo acquisition

---

## Warning Signs of Premature Expansion

Flag these danger signals if a user seems to be expanding too fast:

- **Committing to significant revenue from unproven areas** — e.g., "$2M from Europe next year" when they've never sold there
- **Boiling the ocean** — trying to serve SMB, mid-market, and enterprise across all industries simultaneously
- **No prior experimentation** — launching full-force into a new market without small validation experiments first
- **Underestimating restart cost** — expanding into a new TAM requires restarting product-market fit AND go-to-market fit from scratch

## Expansion Experiment Structure

When a user is ready to run 10% experiments, help them think through:

### 1. Hypothesis
Clearly state what you believe to be true about the new segment — specific enough to be testable.

*Example: "We believe renewable energy companies with 100+ employees face the same compliance
challenges as our current financial customers, but are underserved by existing solutions."*

### 2. Target Definition
Get specific:
- Industry and sub-vertical
- Employee count / revenue range
- Geography
- Specific roles or tech stack requirements

### 3. Experiment Design
- Who runs it? (Often best suited to early sales hires who thrive in ambiguity)
- What's the sales motion? (Modified pitch, specific case studies, relevant channels)
- What product changes, if any, are required? (If >40% of dev resources needed — red flag)

### 4. Resource Allocation
Real numbers: budget, personnel (FTEs), marketing hours, timeline.
Keep it lean but substantial enough to yield real data.

### 5. Success Metrics
Don't measure everything — focus on:
- Meeting volume and quality
- Pipeline development and stage progression
- Early customer success signals (within 60 days of implementation)
- Contract values vs. core ICP average

### 6. Decision Criteria
Define go/no-go thresholds before you start:
- **Scale up**: >90% of targets met
- **Extend**: 70–90% of targets, with clear momentum
- **Pivot**: 40–70% of targets, but promising sub-segment signals
- **Terminate**: <40% of targets, no bright spots — reallocate resources

## People for Expansion Experiments

The right person for expansion experiments often mirrors the first sales hire profile:
- Comfortable with ambiguity and undefined playbooks
- Strong consultative selling skills
- Able to work fluidly with product/engineering on adaptation needs
- Energized by figuring things out from scratch (not following established process)

This is also a retention strategy — early-stage hires who get bored in structured environments
often thrive when given a new market to pioneer.

## The Division Hopping Strategy (Enterprise Expansion)

For users moving into enterprise accounts:

1. **Start with a small foothold deal** — even a $6K pilot gets you through security/procurement and earns the logo
2. **Find and develop an internal champion** who will sponsor you to other divisions
3. **Document the value clearly** — quantifiable business outcomes, not just product features
4. **Replicate the winning use case** — once you find a pattern that works, scale it fast

*Seismic example: A $6K deal with one GE division became $4M/year by expanding across 6 business units.*

**Key insight**: In large organizations, department heads don't share the same goals, politics,
or priorities as your initial champion. Restart discovery with each new stakeholder. Never assume
alignment just because one person loved you.

## Managing ICP Expansion with Investors

Help users set appropriate investor expectations:

- **Don't commit to TAM expansion revenue prematurely** — you can *describe* the long-term vision without locking in numbers from unproven segments
- **Show conviction through experiments, not promises** — metrics like meetings, pipeline, and deal progression tell the story before revenue does
- **Investors need to believe you can get to a billion-dollar market** — not that you've already proven it in year one

The mistake: founders reaching end-of-year targets, committing to $2M from a new European office
the next year, with no prior experimentation or validated product-market fit there.

## When to Do a Full ICP Revision

Versus ongoing refinement, a full ICP re-analysis is warranted when:

- Moving upmarket (e.g., SMB → mid-market → enterprise)
- Entering a new geography as a primary market
- Significant product pivot that changes who gets the most value
- ~$10M ARR with 2–3 years of renewal data (enough to do an LTV-rooted ICP analysis)
- Success criteria shifts from top-line growth (ACV/ARR) to retention and LTV

When ICP changes, update all team playbooks simultaneously:
- Marketing: lead scoring models, ad targeting, content, web personalization
- Sales: outbound lists, qualification criteria, compensation/quotas
- Customer Success: prioritization, renewal focus, expansion targets
- Product: roadmap priorities to serve new segment's needs

---

## Distinguishing Distractions from Real Opportunities

When a promising customer falls outside the current ICP, help users evaluate:

1. **How much does the product need to change?** If <40% of dev resources → worth exploring. If major rebuild required → likely a distraction.
2. **Is there a repeatable pattern?** One interesting customer = anecdote. Three with the same use case = signal.
3. **Is there clear, quantifiable business value?** Gut feel isn't enough. Interview the customer — what specific outcomes changed?
4. **Can you replicate it fast?** If yes and the pattern is clear, consider running a small dedicated experiment.
